\section{Introduction}

It is widely acknowledged that the demand for computational power has
been steadily increasing in the recent years, driven both by advances in
high-performance computing (HPC) applications and the rapid development of
artificial intelligence (AI) applications. Recent empirical studies
highlight that large-scale AI-models training can impose significant
power requirements at the node level, underscoring the growing importance
of energy management in computational infrastructures \citep{Latif_2025}.
Additionaly, international assessments show that digital infrastructures
will continue to exert considerable pressure on global energy systems \citep{IEA2025_AI_Energy}.

This trend is further accentuated by the global emphasis on sustainable
development, where the efficient utilization of energy and the
minimization of environmental impact are increasingly prioritized. In
HPC environments, energy-aware practices have emerged as critical design
criteria, as evidenced by initiatives such as the Green500 ranking and
related analytical studies \citep{GreenHPC}. In order to formulate
effective optimization strategies, regulatory guidelines, and operational
policies, it is essential to obtain precise measurements of energy
consumption across various system components, including central
processing units (CPUs), graphics processing units (GPUs), memory, and
interconnects.

Several software frameworks and tools have been developed to facilitate
the reliable collection of power and energy data (and also other metrics, out of scope of this work).
Representative examples include
\emph{EMA} \footcite{EMA_git},
\emph{Scaphandre} \footcite{Scaphandre},
\emph{Alumet} \footcite{Alumet},
\emph{EAR} \footcite{EAR}, and
\emph{ClusterCockpit} \footcite{ClusterCockpit}.
Each of these tools aims to integrate energy measurement
capabilities within broader performance monitoring and management
workflows.

Among the components of modern computing systems, CPUs occupy a central
role in both HPC and cloud environments, so understanding their
energy characteristics is of paramount importance, particularly in the
context of the evolving CPU market. Although Intel has historically
maintained a dominant position in server and desktop segments, AMD has
achieved notable gains over the past decade. Contemporary market analyses
indicate that AMD has captured a substantial share of both server and
desktop markets, with penetration exceeding 40\% among consumer systems
\citep{NetworkWorld2025_Q1ServerShare}. Moreover, economic
research underscores the strategic significance of competitive dynamics
and innovation within the microprocessor industry \citep{GoettlerGordon2009_Competition}.

Energy measurement of CPUs is typically facilitated through hardware- and
firmware-/software- level interfaces exposed to the operating system kernels. Intel
platforms predominantly employ the Running Average Power Limit (RAPL)
mechanism, which provides estimations of energy consumption for the CPU
package and its subcomponents (like DRAM and such). AMD processors offer a
variety of telemetry mechanisms aside, among which the \texttt{ryzen\_smu}
driver provides low-level access to power measurement registers. In the same time
most of the modern AMD CPU model families also support RAPL
(with some slight differences in realization). Each interface presents distinct trade-offs
in terms of measurement resolution, accuracy, accessibility, and operational overhead,
thereby motivating systematic comparative evaluation.

The objective of the present study is to perform a rigorous comparative
analysis of energy measurement interfaces available on AMD CPUs,
emphasizing the evaluation of the \texttt{ryzen\_smu} interface and its comparison
against AMD's realization of RAPL. By characterizing their accuracy,
resolution, andpractical applicability, this work aims to inform the selection
of energy monitoring techniques that support both high-fidelity measurement and
energy-efficient computing practices.
