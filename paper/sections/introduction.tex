\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}

It is widely acknowledged that the demand for computational power has
been steadily increasing in the recent years, driven both by advances in
high-performance computing \gls{HPC} applications and the rapid development of
\gls{AI}. Recent  studies show that large-scale
AI-models training can impose significant power requirements at the node
level, highlighting the growing importance of energy management in
computational infrastructures \citep{Latif_2025}. Additionally,
international assessments show that digital infrastructures will continue
to exert considerable pressure on global energy systems
\citep{IEA2025_AI_Energy}.

This trend is further accentuated by the global emphasis on sustainable
development, where the efficient utilization of energy and the
minimization of environmental impact are increasingly prioritized. In
\gls{HPC} environments energy-aware practices have emerged as critical design
criteria: evidenced by initiatives such as the \emph{Green500} ranking and
related analytical studies \citep{GreenHPC}. In order to formulate
effective optimization strategies, regulatory guidelines, and operational
policies, it is essential to obtain precise measurements of energy
consumption across various computational system components, including \gls{CPU}s,
\gls{GPU}s, memory, specific network-attached hardware, and interconnects.

Several software frameworks and tools have been developed to facilitate
the reliable collection of power and energy data (and also other
sensor-type metrics, out of scope of this work, such as temperature for
example). Representative examples include
\emph{EMA} \footcite{EMA_git},
\emph{Scaphandre} \footcite{Scaphandre},
\emph{Alumet} \footcite{Alumet},
\emph{EAR} \footcite{EAR}, and
\emph{ClusterCockpit} \footcite{ClusterCockpit}.
Each of these tools aims to integrate energy measurement
capabilities within broader performance monitoring and management
workflows whiles having various subsets of features and/or
limitations.

Among the components of modern computing systems, \gls{CPU}s occupy a central
role in both \gls{HPC} and \emph{Cloud} environments, so comprehension
of their energy characteristics is of paramount importance, particularly
in the context of evolving CPU market. Although Intel has historically
maintained a dominant position in server and desktop segments, AMD has
achieved notable gains over the past decade. Contemporary market analyses
indicate that AMD has captured a substantial share of both server and
desktop markets, with penetration exceeding 40\% among consumer systems
\citep{NetworkWorld2025_Q1ServerShare}. Moreover, some economic
researches highlight the strategic significance of competitive dynamics
and innovation within the microprocessor industry \citep{GoettlerGordon2009_Competition}.

Energy measurement of \gls{CPU}s is typically facilitated through hardware- and
firmware-/software- level interfaces exposed to the operating system kernels.
Intel platforms predominantly employ the \gls{RAPL} mechanism, which provides
estimations of energy consumption for the CPU package and its subdomains,
mitigating security risks (such as power-based side channel attacks) by
including filtering behaviour for model-specific registers \cite{IntelRAPL}.

AMD processors offer a variety of telemetry mechanisms and \gls{SMU}s aside,
among which the \texttt{ryzen\_smu} driver provides low-level access to power
measurement \gls{MSR}s. In the same time most of the modern AMD \gls{CPU} model
families also support \gls{RAPL} (with some slight differences in realization).

Each interface presents distinct trade-offs in terms of measurements
accuracy, accessibility, and operational overhead. This motivates a
systematic comparative evaluation.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The landscape of \gls{CPU} energy measurement interfaces on AMD platforms is
characterized by the coexistence of vendor-specific solutions and
adaptations of mechanisms originally designed for Intel \gls{CPU}s. In
particular, the availability of both the \texttt{ryzen\_smu} interface
and AMD's realization of the \gls{RAPL} mechanism introduces uncertainty in the
selection of appropriate tools for high-fidelity measurement and
integration with existing monitoring frameworks. While \gls{RAPL} is widely
adopted in academic and industrial contexts, its port to AMD hardware
differs in certain aspects and has its specific limitations. Conversely, the
\texttt{ryzen\_smu} driver offers access to low-level telemetry, yet its
practical benefits over \gls{RAPL} remain largely unexplored.

\subsection{Research Questions}

To address this gap, the present work formulates the following research
questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item What are the advantages and limitations of using the
  \texttt{ryzen\_smu} interface for energy monitoring on AMD \gls{CPU}s in
  comparison with the AMD implementation of \gls{RAPL}?
  \item How do the two interfaces differ in terms of measurement accuracy,
  resolution, and stability under various workloads?
  \item What is the relative overhead associated with each interface, and
  how does it impact their suitability for integration into performance
  monitoring and management frameworks and tools?
  \item In which scenarios could AMD-specific interfaces provide a tangible
  advantage over \gls{RAPL} (e.g., higher granularity, lower latency, or
  integration simplicity)?
\end{itemize}
