\chapter*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}


It is widely acknowledged that the demand for computational power has
been steadily increasing in the recent years, driven both by advances in
high-performance computing (HPC) applications and the rapid development of
artificial intelligence (AI). Recent  studies show that large-scale
AI-models training can impose significant power requirements at the node
level, highlighting the growing importance of energy management in
computational infrastructures \citep{Latif_2025}. Additionally,
international assessments show that digital infrastructures will continue
to exert considerable pressure on global energy systems
\citep{IEA2025_AI_Energy}.

This trend is further accentuated by the global emphasis on sustainable
development, where the efficient utilization of energy and the
minimization of environmental impact are increasingly prioritized. In
HPC environments energy-aware practices have emerged as critical design
criteria: evidenced by initiatives such as the \emph{Green500} ranking and
related analytical studies \citep{GreenHPC}. In order to formulate
effective optimization strategies, regulatory guidelines, and operational
policies, it is essential to obtain precise measurements of energy
consumption across various computational system components, including central
processing units (CPUs), graphics processing units (GPUs), memory, specific 
network-attached hardware, and interconnects.

Several software frameworks and tools have been developed to facilitate
the reliable collection of power and energy data (and also other
sensor-type metrics, out of scope of this work, such as temperature for
example). Representative examples include
\emph{EMA} \footcite{EMA_git},
\emph{Scaphandre} \footcite{Scaphandre},
\emph{Alumet} \footcite{Alumet},
\emph{EAR} \footcite{EAR}, and
\emph{ClusterCockpit} \footcite{ClusterCockpit}.
Each of these tools aims to integrate energy measurement
capabilities within broader performance monitoring and management
workflows whiles having various subsets of features and/or
limitations.

Among the components of modern computing systems, CPUs occupy a central
role in both \emph{HPC} and \emph{Cloud} environments, so comprehension
of their energy characteristics is of paramount importance, particularly
in the context of evolving CPU market. Although Intel has historically
maintained a dominant position in server and desktop segments, AMD has
achieved notable gains over the past decade. Contemporary market analyses
indicate that AMD has captured a substantial share of both server and
desktop markets, with penetration exceeding 40\% among consumer systems
\citep{NetworkWorld2025_Q1ServerShare}. Moreover, some economic
researches highlight the strategic significance of competitive dynamics
and innovation within the microprocessor industry \citep{GoettlerGordon2009_Competition}.

Energy measurement of CPUs is typically facilitated through hardware- and
firmware-/software- level interfaces exposed to the operating system kernels.
Intel platforms predominantly employ the Running Average Power Limit (RAPL)
mechanism, which provides estimations of energy consumption for the CPU
package and its subdomains (like DRAM and such) mitigating security risks 
(such as power-based side channel attacks) by including filtering
behaviour for model-specific registers \cite{IntelRAPL}.
AMD processors offer a variety of telemetry mechanisms aside, among which
the \texttt{ryzen\_smu} driver provides low-level access to power
measurement registers. In the same time most of the modern AMD CPU model
families also support RAPL (with some slight differences in realization).
Each interface presents distinct trade-offs in terms of measurement
resolution, accuracy, accessibility, and operational overhead, thereby
motivating systematic comparative evaluation.

The objective of the present study is to perform a rigorous comparative
analysis of energy measurement interfaces available on AMD CPUs,
emphasizing the evaluation of the \texttt{ryzen\_smu} interface and its
comparison against AMD's realization of RAPL. By characterizing their
accuracy, resolution, and practical applicability, this work aims to
inform the selection of energy monitoring techniques that support both
high-fidelity measurement and energy-efficient computing practices.
